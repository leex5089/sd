Introduction

  • The mertis of single-sex (SS) schooling have been a long debated topic
    • No clear consensus in the effects of SS on academic outcomes:
      • Meta-analysis finds larger portion of studies finds positive effect (Mael et al., 2005)
      • Recent quasi-experimental studies find positive effect (Lee et al., 2014; Jackson 2012, 2019; Park, Behrman and Choi, 2013; Dustmann, Ku et al., 2018)
      • Critics argue, positive effect of SS on academic outcomes (Halpern et al., 2011):
        • Weak scientific evidence
        • Negative consequences (gender stereotyping and sexism)
    • Threats to causal inference
      • Non-random selection (Mael et al., 2005; Halpern et al., 2011)
      • Potential systematic difference between SS and Co-educational schools (Teacher, Curriculum and Budget)

Equalization Policy in Seoul, South Korea

  • Randomly assigns middle and high school students within school-districts of their residence
  • Implemented since the 1970s
  • Designed to provide a homogeneous educational environment
    • Prohibit schools from selecting students based on academic performance or SES
    • Reduce excessive competition for middle school entrance exam
  • Compliance is high
    • Applies to all middle and high school students in Seoul
    • If don't like the assigned school, whole family has to re-locate to other school districts, where they are again randomly assigned to schools within school district
  • Does the data provide evidence to support that the policy is implemented randomly?
    • Previous studies lacks the evidence, due to use of cross-sectional (population) data (Park, Behrman and Choi (2013; Dustmann, Ku et al., 2018; Sohn, 2016; Lee et al., 2014).

Research Question & Contribution of this Study

  • What is the effect of single-sex school on students' academic and nonacademic outcomes among middle school students?
  • Contribution
    • This is the first study that uses within-student variation to estimate the effect of SS school
    • this paper examines the impact of SS education on non-academic outcomes.
    • This is the first study that examines the impact of Single-Sex (SS) schooling on students during the transition to SS middle schools within the Korean context.

Data

  • Seoul Education Longitudinal Study (SELS):
    • Annually-surveyed
    • Six waves of survey data (4th grade in 2010..9th grade in 2016)
    • One cohort data
    • Samples about 3,500 students
    • Two-stage cluster random sampling method, randomly selected 289 schools in the city of Seoul and two classes within each selected school.
    • Student's educational environment, acheivement, and also collected from parents, teachers, and schools.

Identification Strategy

  • Matching on pre-treated observables (residential or other unobserved sorting is potentially possible)
  • Two-way Fixed Effects Model (Difference-in-differences estimator)
    • to eliminate further unobserved heterogeneity that might be present

      • \begin{align*} y_{it} = \alpha +\beta SS_{it} + X_{it}\delta + \tau_{t}+ \gamma_{i} + \epsilon_{it} \end{align*}


      • $Y_{it}$ is the school outcomes for student i at time t.
      • $SS_{it}$ is a binary time indicator for pre- and post-school type transition from elementary school to middle SS school for student i.
      • $X_{it}$ is the vector of time-varying student characteristics.
      • $\tau_{t}$ is time dummies that captures time-trends.
      • $\gamma_{i}$ controls for the time-constant unobserved individual level. characteristics
      • $\epsilon_{it}$ is an error term assumed to be i.i.d.

Conclusion 1/2

  • This study examines effect of SS schooling in Middle school on student outcomes using student-level panel data from Seoul, South Korea
  • This is the first study that uses within-student variation in the academic achievement to identify causal effect of SS school
  • Preliminary results suggest:
    • SS schooling have positive effect for girls while smaller and statistically insignificant effect was found for boys
    • Similar findings in the peer effects literature: positive effect of increasing share of female students on academic outcomes (Eren, 2017; Whitmore, 2005; Lu and Anderson, 2015)

Conclusion 2/2

  • Based on student and parents response on multiple questions on their school experience, data suggests that the SS schools:
    • Provide environment that is more academically focused:
      • less disorderly behaviour of peers during class periods
      • less bullying between students
    • Students spend less time on smart phones
    • Parents are more satisfied with school safety, teacher and peer relationships
  • Reduced distraction from "adolescent subculture" (Coleman, 1961)
